What Affects Car Insurance Rates?

Car insurance rates are calculated by combining dozens of risk factors into a single premium. Insurers are trying to predict the likelihood you'll file a claim and what that claim will cost. Understanding which factors carry the most weight helps you make decisions that reduce your premium over time — and know when a rate increase is a red flag worth shopping around.

The national average car insurance cost is around $2,200–$2,600 per year for full coverage as of 2026, according to rate surveys from U.S. News and ValuePenguin, though individual premiums range from under $900 to over $4,000 depending on the driver, vehicle, and location. That range reflects how dramatically the risk factors below can shift your rate.

Age: The Biggest Risk Factor

Age is the single most heavily weighted factor for most insurers. Drivers under 25 — especially males under 25 — pay 50–100% more than middle-aged drivers with identical records. Teen drivers added to a parent's policy can double the family's premium. Rates typically peak in the 16–19 range and fall steadily through the mid-20s as insurers see accident data improve with experience.

Rates dip to their lowest for drivers in the 35–64 range with clean records. After 65, rates begin to rise again slightly as reaction time and vision issues become statistical risk factors, though this increase is far less dramatic than the youth surcharge.

Driving Record: Accidents and Violations

A single at-fault accident typically raises your premium 30–50% for 3–5 years depending on your insurer and state. A DUI can increase rates 70–150% and may cause your current insurer to non-renew your policy. Multiple speeding tickets within a three-year window can add 20–40% to your base rate. Insurers look at your 3–5 year driving history when setting rates.

The flip side: a clean record earns you good driver discounts (typically 10–15%) and keeps your base rate at its lowest. Every year without a claim or ticket moves you further into the preferred driver tier.

Location: More Impact Than Most People Realize

Where you live can affect your rate as much as your driving history. High-traffic urban areas have more accidents per mile driven. High-theft-rate cities add to comprehensive coverage costs. States with high litigation rates (Florida, Michigan, California) have structurally higher insurance costs. Michigan's no-fault insurance system has historically produced the highest average premiums in the country.

Even within a city, your ZIP code matters. Moving from a dense urban neighborhood to a suburban ZIP code with the same car can reduce your premium by 10–25%.

Coverage Level: Liability vs. Full Coverage

Liability-only insurance covers damage you cause to others — other cars, property, medical bills — but nothing for your own vehicle. It's the legal minimum required in most states. Full coverage adds collision (covers your car in accidents regardless of fault) and comprehensive (covers theft, weather damage, fire, animals). Full coverage costs roughly 2–3x more than liability alone.

The decision to carry full coverage usually comes down to your car's value. If your car is worth less than $6,000–$8,000, the annual premium cost for collision and comprehensive may exceed what you'd collect in a total-loss claim. A general rule: if your annual premium for collision and comprehensive exceeds 10% of your car's value, dropping those coverages and self-insuring makes financial sense.

Deductible: How It Changes Your Premium

Your deductible is the amount you pay out of pocket before insurance kicks in on a claim. Common deductibles are $500 and $1,000. Raising your deductible from $500 to $1,000 typically reduces your collision and comprehensive premium by 10–20%. Raising it to $2,000 can cut those coverages by 20–30%. The trade-off: you're taking on more financial risk per claim. This strategy only makes sense if you have the savings to cover the higher deductible without stress.

How to Lower Your Car Insurance Premium

The most impactful step is shopping your coverage annually. Most drivers who switch insurers save 10–25% without changing any coverage. Bundling auto with homeowners or renters insurance typically reduces both policies by 5–15%. Ask about low-mileage discounts if you drive under 7,500 miles per year. Telematics programs (usage-based insurance) that monitor your driving via app can reduce premiums 10–30% for safe drivers.

Maintaining a clean record is the highest-leverage long-term action — avoiding even a single at-fault accident saves more on lifetime premiums than almost any discount program.
