How This Comparison Works

A cosigner doesn't change your loan amount or term — it changes the APR a lender is willing to offer, because the lender is now underwriting two credit profiles instead of one. This calculator amortizes the same loan amount and term at your solo APR and at a cosigned APR, so you can see the real dollar difference in monthly payment and total interest before you ask someone to cosign.

Two Worked Examples

Example 1 — thin/damaged credit: $22,000 financed over 60 months. Solo at 14% APR runs about $511.90/month with roughly $8,714 in total interest. With a cosigner who has strong credit, the same loan at 7% APR drops to about $435.63/month with roughly $4,138 in interest. That's about $76/month and $4,577 in total interest saved.

Example 2 — already decent credit: $28,000 financed over 60 months. Solo at 9.5% APR runs about $588.05/month with roughly $7,283 in interest. With a cosigner at 6.5% APR, that drops to about $547.85/month with roughly $4,871 in interest — about $40/month and $2,412 in total interest saved. The gain shrinks as your own credit improves, since the APR gap a cosigner can realistically close gets smaller.

When a Cosigner Is Worth Asking For

A cosigner tends to make the biggest difference when your credit is thin (little to no credit history) or has recent negative marks, since that's when lenders quote the widest APR spreads. If the calculator shows a gap of 3+ percentage points between your solo and cosigned rate, the savings are usually large enough to justify the conversation. If your own credit already qualifies for a competitive market rate, a cosigner may not move the needle much — get quotes both ways before asking.

What a Cosigner Actually Takes On

A cosigner is legally responsible for the entire loan, not a portion of it, if payments are missed — even though they typically have no ownership stake in the vehicle and never drive it. The loan also appears on the cosigner's credit report and counts against their debt-to-income ratio, which can affect their own ability to borrow (a mortgage application, for example) while the loan is open. Late or missed payments hurt both credit files equally. This is worth discussing openly with a prospective cosigner before applying — it's a real, shared financial commitment, not a formality.

The Most Common Mistake With This Decision

Borrowers often ask someone to cosign without first getting a solo pre-approval quote to compare against — which means neither side knows whether the cosigner is actually needed, or how much difference they'll really make. Get pre-approved on your own first, then run both APRs through this calculator before deciding whether to involve a cosigner at all.

What This Calculator Doesn't Account For

It compares APR and payment only — it doesn't factor in origination fees some lenders charge for cosigned loans, the cosigner's own debt-to-income capacity (which can still cause a lender to decline even with a cosigner), or how difficult it may be to remove the cosigner later, since most lenders only allow that through a full refinance once you qualify solo. Run your own numbers here as a starting point, then confirm the actual quoted terms with the lender.
