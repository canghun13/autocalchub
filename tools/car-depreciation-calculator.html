How Does Car Depreciation Work?

Depreciation is the loss in a vehicle's value over time, and it's the single largest cost of car ownership that most buyers don't account for when buying new. A $40,000 car that's worth $22,000 after five years has cost you $18,000 in depreciation alone — more than most people pay in fuel and maintenance combined over that same period. Understanding depreciation helps you make smarter decisions about when to buy, what to buy, and when to sell.

Depreciation follows a predictable but front-loaded curve. The steepest losses happen in the first three years, after which the rate slows considerably. This is why buying a 2–3 year old used car is often the most financially efficient move in the car market.

Year-by-Year Depreciation Breakdown

Year one is the most painful for new car buyers: the average vehicle loses 15–25% of its purchase price in the first 12 months, based on depreciation benchmarks tracked by Kelley Blue Book, Edmunds, and iSeeCars. Much of that drop happens the moment you drive off the lot — the car transitions from "new" to "used" status instantly, which eliminates the manufacturer warranty transfer value and shifts the buyer pool to used-car shoppers with lower price expectations.

Years two and three each bring another 10–15% loss. By the end of year three, the typical new car has retained only 55–65% of its original value. Years four through six see 8–12% annual loss as the car enters a more stable phase of its value curve. After year seven, depreciation often slows to 5–8% annually for vehicles that remain reliable and in demand.

Which Vehicles Hold Their Value Best

Toyota and Honda consistently dominate the resale value rankings. The Toyota Tacoma and 4Runner, Honda CR-V, and Subaru Outback regularly retain 55–70% of their value after five years. Jeep Wrangler is an outlier in the off-road segment, holding value unusually well due to strong brand loyalty and limited competition. American full-size trucks (Ford F-150, Chevy Silverado, Ram 1500) also tend to depreciate more slowly than sedans and crossovers.

At the other end, luxury vehicles from BMW, Audi, Mercedes-Benz, and Volvo depreciate rapidly — often retaining only 35–50% of their value after five years. High maintenance costs, technology obsolescence, and a larger pool of cautious used-car buyers who avoid luxury brands all contribute. Electric vehicles present a mixed picture: Teslas have historically held value better than other EVs, but many non-Tesla EVs have depreciated faster than expected as the market has expanded with newer, higher-range models.

Why Depreciation Matters for Financing

When you finance a car, you owe the bank the loan balance regardless of what the car is worth. If you put little money down and take a long loan term (72–84 months), there's a significant window — often the first 2–4 years — where your loan balance exceeds the car's market value. This is called being "upside down" or underwater on the loan.

Being upside down creates real risk: if your car is totaled in an accident, insurance pays market value — not your loan balance. You'd still owe the difference. This is exactly what GAP insurance is designed to cover. Putting 20% down and keeping loan terms to 48–60 months significantly reduces the time you spend underwater.

How to Use This Depreciation Calculator

Enter your car's original purchase price, select the number of years to project, and choose a depreciation rate. Use 20% for year one of a new vehicle, then 12–15% for subsequent years on average vehicles. High-retention vehicles (Toyota, Honda trucks/SUVs) can use 10–12%. Fast-depreciating vehicles (luxury sedans, some EVs) may warrant 18–22%. The resulting schedule shows you the projected value at the end of each year, how much value was lost that year, and the cumulative loss — useful for timing a trade-in or sale.

The Smart Way to Minimize Depreciation Loss

The most effective strategy is buying a vehicle that's 2–3 years old rather than new. You pay retail for the "experienced" version rather than absorbing the steepest part of the depreciation curve yourself. Choosing brands with strong resale value (Toyota, Honda, Subaru, Jeep) keeps more of your money in the vehicle over time. Maintaining service records, avoiding excessive mileage, and keeping the vehicle in good cosmetic condition all reduce the depreciation penalty when you go to sell or trade in.

Mileage Changes the Curve, Not Just the Starting Point

Age and mileage interact rather than working independently. A 3-year-old car with 20,000 miles (well below the roughly 12,000-15,000/year average) typically depreciates more slowly going forward than a 3-year-old car with 60,000 miles, because low mileage signals lower expected future maintenance needs to the next buyer. As a rough guide, each 10,000 miles above the age-adjusted average can reduce resale value by $500-$1,500 depending on the vehicle's price range — on top of the standard age-based depreciation this calculator projects. If you drive well above or below average annual mileage, treat the depreciation rate you select here as a starting point and adjust it based on where your mileage falls relative to your car's age.

New vs. Certified Pre-Owned vs. Used: Where the Curve Hits Hardest

Because depreciation is so heavily front-loaded, the biggest lever you control isn't the brand you choose — it's how new the car is when you buy it. A brand-new car absorbs the steepest single-year loss (15-25%) the moment it's purchased. A certified pre-owned car, typically 1-3 years old, has already absorbed that first-year hit, so you're paying closer to the car's true market value from day one. A traditional used car, often 3+ years old, has moved past the steepest part of the curve entirely and depreciates at the slower, more predictable rate this calculator applies to later years.
