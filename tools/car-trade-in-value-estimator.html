Trade-In vs. Private Sale: Which Is Better?

Dealers typically offer 10–20% less than private party value for your trade-in. That gap exists because the dealer needs to inspect, recondition, and resell the car at a profit. In return, you get convenience: no listings, no test drives with strangers, no waiting for payment.

When trade-in makes sense

Trading in is usually the right choice when your car has issues that would be hard to disclose and negotiate in a private sale, when you need the transaction completed quickly, or when the tax savings from trading in offset the lower value. In many states, you only pay sales tax on the difference between the new car price and your trade-in value — which can save you $500–$2,000 depending on your state and car values.

When private sale makes more sense

If your car is clean, well-maintained, and in demand, private sale can net you $2,000–$5,000 more than a dealer trade-in. Platforms like Facebook Marketplace, Craigslist, and CarGurus make it straightforward to list privately. The tradeoff is time and the effort of coordinating test drives and payment.

Get multiple offers before deciding

Before accepting any dealer trade-in offer, get instant online offers from CarMax, Carvana, and Vroom. These services make firm cash offers that you can use as leverage with the dealer — or simply accept if they're higher. The process takes less than 10 minutes online and gives you real market data on what your car is actually worth today.

What Affects Your Trade-In Value Most?

In order of impact:

  1. Mileage — Every 10,000 miles above average (12,000–15,000/year) reduces value by $500–$1,500
  2. Condition — Going from "good" to "excellent" can add $500–$2,000 for high-demand models
  3. Accident history — Even a minor reported accident typically reduces value 10–15%
  4. Brand and model demand — Toyota Tacomas and Honda CRVs hold value dramatically better than most European luxury sedans
  5. Market timing — Truck and SUV values rise with gas prices; EV values have been volatile

How to maximize your trade-in value

Clean the car thoroughly inside and out before any appraisal. Fix small cosmetic issues if the repair cost is less than the value gain. Have your service records ready — documented maintenance history adds credibility and value. Time your trade-in for spring or early summer when used car demand is seasonally highest.

How this estimator calculates a value

This tool works off your vehicle's estimated base value adjusted by mileage, condition, and accident history — the same factors dealers and appraisal tools weigh most heavily. It's meant to give you a realistic planning range, not a guaranteed offer; actual trade-in offers vary by dealer, region, and current local demand for your specific make and model. Treat the estimate as a starting point for negotiation, and cross-check it against a couple of instant online offers before you walk into a dealership.

Trading in a car you still owe money on

If your trade-in still has a loan balance, the dealer pays off that balance using the trade-in value, and the difference goes toward your new purchase — either as equity that reduces what you finance, or as negative equity that gets added to your new loan if you owe more than the car is worth. Always ask the dealer for the exact payoff amount and trade-in value as separate line items rather than a single bundled number, so you can see clearly whether you're starting the new loan with equity or with debt rolled over.

Documents to bring for an accurate appraisal

Bring your title (or loan payoff information if it's financed), current registration, all sets of keys, and any maintenance or repair records you have. Missing documentation, especially a missing key or lien paperwork, can lower an offer or add delays — appraisers price in the hassle of tracking down what's missing.
