What Is Loan-to-Value (LTV) Ratio? How It Affects Your Car Loan

9 min read · July 2026 · Written by AutoCalcHub Team
Advertisement · Google AdSense

Loan-to-value (LTV) ratio compares how much you're borrowing to what the car is actually worth. It's one of the main numbers a lender looks at before approving your loan and setting your rate — separate from your credit score, and often less talked about, even though it can move your approval odds and APR just as much.

How LTV Is Calculated

LTV = (Loan Amount ÷ Vehicle Value) × 100

If you're financing $28,000 on a car worth $30,000, your LTV is 93.3%. If you're financing $30,000 on that same $30,000 car — no down payment — your LTV is 100%. Anything financed above the car's value (taxes, fees, an extended warranty, or rolled-in negative equity from a previous loan) pushes your LTV above 100%.

LTV RangeWhat It Signals to a Lender
Under 80%Low risk — you have real equity in the car from day one
80–100%Standard — typical for financed purchases with a modest down payment
100–125%Higher risk — often includes rolled-in negative equity, taxes, or add-ons financed into the loan
125%+High risk — many lenders cap financing here or decline; refinancing becomes difficult

Why Lenders Care About LTV

The car is the collateral securing your loan. If you stop paying, the lender's fallback is to repossess and sell the vehicle to recover what's owed. A lower LTV means the car is worth more than the loan balance, so the lender is fully covered even after resale costs and some depreciation. A high LTV means the lender could lose money on repossession — the resale value might not cover the remaining balance — so they price that risk into your rate, or decline the loan if the ratio is too high.

This is a genuinely different lens from your credit score. A borrower with excellent credit but a 100%+ LTV (small down payment, rolled-in negative equity, add-ons financed in) can still face a higher rate than their score alone would suggest, because the collateral risk is separate from the repayment risk.

How LTV Affects Your Rate

Most lenders use LTV tiers as one input into their rate sheet alongside credit score. Crossing from, say, 90% to 105% LTV can move your rate by half a point to a full point, even with identical credit. On a $25,000 loan over 60 months, a 1-point rate increase adds roughly $650-$700 in total interest — a real cost attached purely to how the loan is structured, not to your creditworthiness.

What Pushes Your LTV Higher

  • Small or no down payment — the most direct lever; even 10% down meaningfully lowers your starting LTV compared to 0%
  • Rolled-in negative equity from a trade-in — see our guide on whether rolling in negative equity is worth it, since this is one of the most common ways LTV climbs above 100%
  • Taxes, fees, and add-ons financed into the loan — extended warranties and GAP insurance sold at the dealership are often rolled into the loan amount rather than paid upfront
  • Buying at or above MSRP — if the car's appraised value comes in below what you paid, your effective LTV is higher than the sale price alone would suggest

How This Connects to Refinancing

LTV matters again later if you try to refinance. Most refinance lenders have maximum LTV limits — often around 125-130% — above which they won't approve a new loan, regardless of your credit score. If your current loan's LTV is very high, refinancing may be difficult until you've paid down the balance or the car has had time to appreciate relative to depreciation, or both. This is one reason it's worth checking your current LTV before assuming refinancing will lower your payment — the math only works if a lender is willing to take on the loan at your current ratio.

A Worked Example

Two buyers finance the same $30,000 car. Buyer A puts 15% down ($4,500), financing $25,500 — an LTV of about 85%. Buyer B puts nothing down and rolls in $3,000 of negative equity from a trade-in, financing $33,000 — an LTV of 110%. Even with identical credit scores, Buyer B is likely to see a noticeably higher APR offer, because the lender is financing more than the car is worth with less cushion if something goes wrong. Buyer A, by contrast, has built-in equity from day one and typically qualifies for the lender's best available rate tier.

This is worth keeping in mind separately from the 20/4/10 rule guidance common in car-buying advice — that rule targets a comfortable payment and down payment size for your budget, while LTV is specifically about how a lender views the collateral risk on the loan itself. The two overlap (a 20% down payment naturally produces a lower LTV) but they're answering different questions, and it's the LTV side that shows up directly in your rate sheet.

How to Keep Your LTV Manageable

  • Put down at least 10-20% if possible — this alone does more to control LTV than almost any other factor
  • Avoid financing add-ons (extended warranties, GAP, paint protection) into the loan — pay separately or skip them if they aren't clearly worth it
  • Avoid rolling in negative equity where possible, or minimize it by paying down the old loan first or selling privately instead of trading in
  • Choose a shorter loan term when you can afford the payment — your balance drops faster relative to the car's value, improving your LTV every month rather than just at signing
