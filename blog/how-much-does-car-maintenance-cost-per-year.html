How Much Does Car Maintenance Cost Per Year?

10 min read · Updated June 2026
Advertisement · Google AdSense

The national average for annual car maintenance in 2026 is around $936, according to RepairPal data. But that number hides enormous variation. A 3-year-old Toyota Corolla driven 12,000 miles a year might cost $400–$500 to maintain. A 10-year-old BMW 5 Series with 100,000 miles can easily exceed $2,000. Understanding what drives the difference helps you budget accurately and avoid surprises.

What's Included in "Maintenance Cost"

Annual maintenance encompasses two categories. Scheduled maintenance covers the predictable services in your owner's manual: oil changes, tire rotations, brake pad replacements, air filters, fluid flushes, and spark plugs. Unscheduled repairs cover the things that fail unexpectedly — a battery, an alternator, a wheel bearing, a CV axle. Both categories are included in the figures below because in practice, the line between them blurs as vehicles age.

Annual Cost by Vehicle Age

Age is the most reliable predictor of maintenance cost. As vehicles accumulate mileage, more components reach the end of their service life simultaneously.

The jump from year 8 to year 9+ is often steep. This is when timing components, water pumps, and suspension bushings all start failing around the same time. A single major repair — transmission, engine work, air conditioning compressor — can add $1,500–$3,000 to an otherwise average year.

A Honda owner spends roughly $583/year on average; a Porsche owner spends around $1,623 — nearly three times as much, according to RepairPal data. The gap isn't just parts cost. European luxury vehicles require specialized technicians, proprietary diagnostic tools, and dealer-exclusive components that inflate labor rates across every service visit.

Over a 10-year ownership period, the maintenance cost difference between a Toyota Camry (~$5,000 total) and a BMW 5 Series (~$12,000 total) is roughly $7,000. That gap is real money that rarely shows up in the purchase decision.

EVs: Genuinely Lower Maintenance

Electric vehicles have significantly lower maintenance costs than gas-powered equivalents — and the gap is real, not marketing. EVs eliminate: engine oil changes (saving $130–$200/year), spark plugs, timing belt/chain, transmission fluid, and most exhaust system work. Regenerative braking also reduces brake pad wear substantially, often extending pad life to 100,000+ miles.

The remaining EV maintenance: tire rotations, cabin air filter, wiper blades, brake fluid checks, and windshield washer fluid. Annual cost typically runs $300–$500. The one major wildcard is battery replacement, but most EVs are within their 8–10 year battery warranty at the point where degradation becomes noticeable.

How to Budget for Maintenance

The most practical approach: treat maintenance as a monthly expense by dividing your annual estimate by 12 and setting it aside in a dedicated account. For a 5-year-old Japanese sedan, $60–$70/month covers most scheduled maintenance and leaves a buffer for unscheduled repairs. For an older vehicle or a European brand, $100–$150/month is more realistic.

Having the money available when service is due prevents the deferred-maintenance trap — where people skip oil changes or delay brake work because the cash isn't there, which leads to far more expensive repairs down the line.

High-Mileage Vehicles: What Changes at 100,000 Miles

Crossing 100,000 miles doesn't automatically mean expensive problems — but it does mean the major maintenance events that many owners defer have typically arrived. Timing belts (on vehicles that have them) are typically due between 60,000–100,000 miles. Failure during operation can destroy the engine on interference-design engines, turning a $600–$900 scheduled service into a $4,000–$8,000 engine rebuild. Checking and addressing this before purchase on any used vehicle is essential.

Water pumps, serpentine belts, and thermostats are commonly replaced at or near 100,000 miles — often bundled with a timing belt service to save labor cost. Suspension components (struts, shocks, control arm bushings) are typically fatigued by this mileage and begin affecting ride quality and tire wear. Wheel bearings become a more common failure point after 90,000–120,000 miles.

Dealer vs Independent Mechanic: The Cost Gap

Where you have your car serviced can be as important as what's being serviced. Dealership labor rates typically run $120–$200/hour in most markets. Independent shops average $75–$130/hour. For a job that takes 2 hours of labor, that difference can be $90–$140 per visit — every visit.

The gap matters most for European luxury brands. A BMW dealership charges $180–$220/hour in most urban markets. An independent shop specializing in German vehicles charges $110–$150/hour for the same work. Over a year of regular maintenance, the savings from an independent shop can be $500–$800 on a higher-maintenance vehicle.

For routine services (oil changes, filters, tire rotations), chains like Jiffy Lube or Firestone offer competitive pricing, particularly for non-luxury vehicles. For anything more complex — diagnosis, engine work, electrical systems — an experienced independent shop with quality technicians typically provides better value than either chains or dealerships.

Extended Warranties: Worth It?

Extended warranties (also called vehicle service contracts) are sold aggressively by dealers, often at $1,500–$3,500, and promise to cover unexpected repairs after the factory warranty expires. Whether they're worth it depends heavily on the vehicle and the contract terms.

For high-reliability vehicles like a Toyota Camry or Honda CR-V, the probability of a major covered repair is low enough that the warranty is often pure profit for the seller. For vehicles with known reliability issues, the warranty can be valuable — but those same vehicles often have exclusions for their most common failure modes buried in the fine print.

Before purchasing: read the exclusion list carefully (everything not covered), verify the deductible per repair visit, check whether you can use any licensed shop or only dealer-affiliated shops, and research the claims reputation of the warranty provider. Many are sold by third-party companies, not the manufacturer, and have inconsistent claim payment practices.

The Bottom Line

The $936 national average is a useful starting point, but your actual number depends heavily on what you drive, how old it is, and how you've maintained it. Japanese vehicles and EVs sit well below average. European luxury vehicles sit well above it. The most expensive maintenance decision most people make is buying a vehicle without understanding what it will cost to keep running — and then getting surprised by a $1,500 repair at 80,000 miles that was entirely predictable.
