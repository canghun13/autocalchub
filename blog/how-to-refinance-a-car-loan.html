How to Refinance a Car Loan: Step-by-Step Guide

9 min read · Updated July 2026 · Written by AutoCalcHub Team

Refinancing a car loan means replacing your current loan with a new one — ideally at a lower interest rate, a better term, or both. It's one of the more underused tools in personal finance. Done at the right time, refinancing can save hundreds or even thousands of dollars without requiring you to change vehicles. Here's a complete walkthrough of when it makes sense and how to actually do it.

What Refinancing Actually Does

When you refinance, a new lender pays off your existing auto loan in full. You then make payments to the new lender under new terms. Your car, your driving routine, and your insurance don't change. The only things that change are who you pay and what you pay each month.

Refinancing can lower your monthly payment in two ways: by securing a lower interest rate (which reduces total cost), or by extending the loan term (which spreads payments out but usually increases total interest). Understanding which goal you're after matters — they have different financial outcomes.

When Refinancing Makes Sense

Your credit score has improved

This is the most common and compelling reason to refinance. If you took out your original loan with a 620 credit score and you're now at 700, you may qualify for a rate that's 3–5 percentage points lower. On a $20,000 loan, that's a meaningful savings over 48 months.

Interest rates have dropped

Auto loan rates move with broader market conditions. If you took out your loan during a rate spike and rates have since come down, refinancing lets you capture that improvement without selling or trading the car.

You got a dealership rate that wasn't competitive

Dealer financing is often marked up. Dealerships act as intermediaries between you and a lender and typically add a margin to the rate they're offered — sometimes 1–3 percentage points. If you accepted dealer financing in a hurry, there's a good chance you can find a better rate by shopping directly.

Your monthly payment is straining your budget

If your financial situation has changed, refinancing to a longer term can lower your monthly payment — though you'll pay more in total interest. This is a legitimate option when cash flow matters more than total cost, but go in with your eyes open.

When Refinancing Probably Isn't Worth It

  • You're near the end of your loan — if you have 6–12 months left, most of your interest is already paid. The savings won't justify the application effort and any fees.
  • Your car is very old or high-mileage — many lenders have minimum vehicle requirements (typically under 10 years old and under 100,000–125,000 miles). Older vehicles may not qualify.
  • You're underwater on your loan — lenders typically won't refinance for more than the vehicle is worth. If you have significant negative equity, refinancing may not be an option.
  • The rate difference is minimal — refinancing for 0.5% less on a $10,000 balance with 24 months remaining saves maybe $60 total. Not worth the credit inquiry and paperwork.

A useful rule of thumb: if you can reduce your rate by at least 1–2 percentage points and you have more than 12 months left on the loan, refinancing is likely worth exploring.

How to Refinance: Step by Step

  1. Check your current loan details Log into your lender's account or call them to get your exact payoff amount, remaining term, and current interest rate. The payoff amount is what you owe today — it may differ slightly from your remaining balance due to how interest accrues.
  2. Check your credit score Pull your credit report from AnnualCreditReport.com and your FICO score from your bank or credit card issuer. This tells you which rate tier you're likely to qualify for before you start shopping. If your score has dropped since you got your original loan, refinancing may not help.
  3. Gather your documents You'll typically need: driver's license, proof of income (pay stubs or tax returns), proof of insurance, your current loan account number and payoff amount, and your car's VIN, mileage, and title information.
  4. Shop at least 3 lenders Apply to your current bank or credit union, at least one online lender (LightStream, PenFed, and Capital One Auto are commonly used), and one other. Multiple applications within a 14-day window are typically treated as a single inquiry for credit scoring purposes — so don't hold back from comparing.
  5. Compare offers carefully Don't just look at the monthly payment — look at the total cost of the loan (monthly payment × remaining months) and any fees. Some lenders charge origination fees or prepayment penalties. A slightly higher rate with no fees can sometimes beat a lower rate with a $300 fee.
  6. Accept the best offer and complete the application Once you choose a lender, they'll confirm your vehicle details, verify your income and insurance, and process the payoff to your old lender. This typically takes 3–7 business days.
  7. Confirm your old loan is closed After your new loan funds, verify with your old lender that the account shows a zero balance and is marked paid in full. This matters for your credit report and protects you from double-billing errors.

Does Refinancing Hurt Your Credit Score?

Refinancing has a small, temporary effect on your credit score. Shopping multiple lenders within a 14-day window counts as a single hard inquiry — typically a 5–10 point dip that recovers within a few months. Closing your old loan and opening a new one slightly affects your average account age, which is a minor factor in your score. For most borrowers, the financial savings far outweigh any short-term credit impact.

Can You Refinance More Than Once?

Yes, there's no rule against refinancing multiple times. If rates drop significantly after your first refinance, or if your credit score improves further, it's worth checking again. The same cost-benefit analysis applies each time: is the rate improvement large enough and is there enough loan left to justify the effort?

The Bottom Line

Refinancing a car loan is one of the more straightforward ways to reduce a recurring monthly expense. If your credit has improved since your original loan, if you accepted dealer financing without shopping around, or if rates have moved in your favor, it's worth spending an afternoon getting quotes. The process is less complicated than it sounds, and the savings can be substantial — sometimes more than the cost of a month's payment, every year for the remaining life of the loan.
