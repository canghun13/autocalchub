How Much Does It Cost to Fill Up a Car?

9 min read · Updated June 2026
Advertisement · Google AdSense

With gas prices fluctuating constantly, the cost of a fill-up varies a lot depending on your car and where you live. Here's a clear breakdown of what you can expect to pay in 2026.

Average Fill-Up Cost in 2026

The national average gas price in the US in 2026 is around $3.20–$3.60 per gallon for regular unleaded. Most passenger cars have tanks between 12 and 20 gallons, which puts a typical fill-up in the range of $40 to $70 from empty.

Most drivers don't fill up from completely empty. If your tank is half full, divide these numbers by two to get your real fill-up cost at the pump.

Premium vs Regular Gas: Does It Matter?

Most cars run on regular 87 octane. If your car only recommends premium but doesn't require it, regular is fine — you'll see a minor efficiency difference but nothing significant. If your car requires premium (check your owner's manual or the fuel cap), don't skip it. Using regular in a car that requires premium can cause engine knock and long-term damage.

Premium gas typically costs $0.50–$0.80 more per gallon than regular, adding $10–$20 to a full tank.

How Gas Prices Vary by State

Where you live matters a lot. California regularly sees gas prices $1.00–$1.50 above the national average due to state taxes and special fuel blends. States like Texas, Mississippi, and Oklahoma are typically among the cheapest. If you're near a state border, it can be worth crossing over to fill up.

How Many Times a Month Do You Fill Up?

The average American drives about 1,200 miles per month. If your car gets 28 MPG, that's roughly 43 gallons per month — around 2–3 fill-ups. At $3.40/gallon, that's about $146/month in gas for an average driver.

Fill-Up Cost vs Monthly Fuel Budget

Knowing the cost per fill-up is useful, but your real fuel budget is based on how often you fill up. Most drivers with a midsize car fill up once every 1–2 weeks. At $50–$60 per fill-up, that's $100–$240/month in fuel — often more than people estimate when budgeting for a new car.

To reduce how often you fill up, focus on fuel economy: smooth acceleration, proper tire inflation, and reducing unnecessary trips at peak hours all make a measurable difference. Our fuel cost calculator lets you estimate your monthly spend based on your car's MPG and weekly mileage.

Does Fuel Grade Matter for Your Wallet?

For cars that require premium fuel, there's no way around the cost difference — skipping premium on a required-premium engine can cause damage that costs far more than the gas savings. For cars that only recommend premium (not require), regular 87 octane is fine in most conditions with a small efficiency trade-off. Check your owner's manual or the fuel cap label to confirm which applies to your car.

How to Spend Less at the Pump

  • Use GasBuddy or the Waze app to find the cheapest station nearby
  • Fill up on Mondays or Tuesdays — prices tend to rise toward the weekend
  • Pay with a cash-back credit card that rewards gas purchases (some give 3–5% back)
  • Keep tires properly inflated — low pressure hurts fuel economy
  • Avoid filling up right after a fuel delivery — stirred sediment can clog your filter

Annual Fuel Cost by Vehicle and Mileage

Understanding the annual cost of fuel helps you make smarter vehicle purchase decisions. At $3.40/gallon and 15,000 miles/year, a compact car getting 35 MPG costs about $1,457/year in fuel. A midsize SUV at 26 MPG runs $1,962/year. A full-size pickup at 20 MPG costs $2,550/year. Over a 5-year ownership period, the difference between a compact and a truck is over $5,000 just in fuel.

These annual costs are sensitive to gas price changes. If prices spike to $4.50/gallon — not unusual during supply disruptions — the same truck owner pays $3,375/year instead of $2,550, an increase of $825. High-mileage drivers in low-MPG vehicles feel price spikes the most.

Gas Price Variation by State

Where you live dramatically affects your fill-up cost. California has historically led the nation in gas prices, often running $1.00–$1.50/gallon above the national average due to state excise taxes, environmental surcharges, and California's unique fuel blend requirements (CARB-compliant fuel that costs more to produce). Illinois, Washington, and Pennsylvania also carry above-average prices due to high state fuel taxes.

Texas, Mississippi, Louisiana, and Oklahoma consistently rank among the lowest-priced states due to proximity to refineries, lower state taxes, and competitive local markets. If you live near a state border where the neighboring state has significantly lower gas taxes, filling up across the border can save $0.20–$0.40/gallon on each fill-up — meaningful savings for high-mileage drivers.

Regular vs. Premium: The Real-World Math

Cars that require premium fuel (not just recommend it) have engines tuned to a higher compression ratio that needs higher-octane fuel to prevent pre-ignition (engine knock). These are typically turbocharged engines or performance vehicles. Using regular in a required-premium engine over time can cause knock, reduce power, and in extreme cases cause long-term engine damage — repairs that quickly eclipse any gas savings.

Cars that only recommend premium — a larger category that includes many luxury vehicles — can safely run on regular 87 octane. The ECU (engine control unit) adjusts timing to compensate, which results in a minor reduction in power (usually 5–15 horsepower) and fuel efficiency (1–3% in most conditions). For most drivers, this trade-off is negligible. In hot weather or under heavy load (towing, mountain driving), the performance difference becomes more noticeable.

Diesel: Higher Cost Per Gallon, Better Efficiency

Diesel fuel typically costs $0.20–$0.60 more per gallon than regular gasoline. However, diesel engines are 20–35% more fuel-efficient than comparable gasoline engines, so diesel drivers often pay less per mile than gasoline drivers despite the higher pump price. A diesel pickup getting 28 highway MPG will cost less per highway mile than a gasoline equivalent at 22 MPG, even when diesel costs $0.40/gallon more.

Diesel makes the most economic sense for high-mileage highway drivers, especially in trucks and larger vehicles where diesel efficiency advantages are most pronounced. City driving erodes the diesel advantage because diesel engines are less efficient in stop-and-go conditions relative to their highway advantage.

How Often Do People Fill Up?

Fill-up frequency depends on your tank size and weekly mileage. A compact car with a 12-gallon tank driven 300 miles/week at 35 MPG uses about 8.6 gallons/week and fills up every 10–11 days. A pickup truck with a 26-gallon tank driven the same 300 miles at 20 MPG uses 15 gallons/week and fills up once a week or so.

Most drivers settle into a rhythm of filling up once or twice per week. If you find yourself filling up 3 or more times per week, either your tank is very small, your mileage is very high, or your fuel economy is significantly below where it should be — which might signal a maintenance issue worth investigating.
