EV Lease vs Buy in 2026: How the Math Changed

9 min read · Updated July 2026 · Written by AutoCalcHub Team
Advertisement · Google AdSense

Through 2024 and into 2025, leasing became the default way to get into an EV — not because leases are inherently better, but because a tax-code quirk let leasing companies pass along a discount that buyers couldn't get. That quirk closed on September 30, 2025. If you're comparing an EV lease against a purchase in 2026, the decision now comes down to the same fundamentals that apply to any vehicle — plus a few things that are specific to EVs.

Quick answer: With the federal purchase credit and the lease "loophole" both gone, EVs no longer have a built-in reason to lease over buying. The decision now hinges on ordinary factors: whether you value lower monthly payments and protection from battery/tech depreciation risk (lease), or building equity and avoiding mileage limits (buy) — plus whatever manufacturer incentive is actually on the table for the specific vehicle you're looking at.

Why EV Leasing Took Off (and Why That Changed)

Leased vehicles are classified as commercial vehicles under federal tax law, so leasing companies could claim the Commercial Clean Vehicle Credit (Section 45W) — worth up to $7,500 — without any of the income limits, price caps, or North American assembly rules that applied to the consumer purchase credit. Many captive lenders passed some or all of that credit through as reduced lease cash, which is why EV leasing share climbed sharply and why leased EVs were often the cheaper monthly option even on vehicles that didn't qualify for the purchase credit at all.

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act ended this on the same date as the purchase credit: September 30, 2025. Per current IRS guidance, the Commercial Clean Vehicle Credit is not available for vehicles acquired after that date. If a dealer or ad still implies a "$7,500 off your lease" deal tied to a federal credit, verify the actual numbers — that specific mechanism is gone. What's Actually Different About Leasing vs Buying an EV

With the tax-credit arbitrage removed, EV lease-vs-buy comes down to the same trade-offs that apply to any vehicle, with a couple of EV-specific wrinkles layered on top:

  • Battery and tech risk. EV technology is moving faster than gas-engine technology did. A 3-year-old EV can be meaningfully behind a new model on range and charging speed, which can hit resale value harder than it would for a comparable gas car. Leasing shifts that risk to the leasing company; buying keeps it with you.
  • Depreciation is less predictable. As covered in our electric vs gas true cost comparison, EV resale values vary more by brand and model than gas cars do. If you're not confident in a specific model's resale value, leasing removes that uncertainty entirely.
  • No mileage caps when you buy. Leases typically cap you at 10,000–12,000 miles/year, with per-mile charges beyond that. High-mileage drivers — who also get the biggest fuel-cost benefit from an EV — often come out ahead financially by buying instead.
  • Manufacturer incentives still move the needle. Some automakers and captive lenders have kept EV lease payments competitive with subsidized financing or lease cash of their own, independent of any federal credit. These come and go by model and quarter — compare the actual deal in front of you, not what EV leases used to cost in general.

A Worked Example

Take a $44,000 EV with $4,000 down, financed at 6.9% APR over 60 months if you buy. That works out to a monthly payment of about $790, with roughly $7,410 in total interest over the loan.

Experian's lease-vs-loan data has consistently shown leased EVs running lower per month than financed EVs on comparable models — its Q3 2025 report put the average gap at about $88/month across all EV models, and notably wider (around $175/month) for the most popular models specifically. Applied to this example, a comparable lease on the same vehicle might land somewhere in the roughly $615–$700/month range — but your actual number depends entirely on the automaker's residual value assumption and money factor for that specific model, which vary widely. When Buying Usually Wins

If you plan to keep the vehicle 6+ years, drive more than the typical lease mileage allowance, or are looking at a model with a track record of strong resale value (Tesla has generally held up better than most legacy-automaker EVs), buying tends to make more financial sense now that there's no credit specifically rewarding the lease structure. You also may qualify for the new federal car-loan interest deduction if the vehicle is new and assembled in the US — a modest but real offset that only applies to a purchase, not a lease.

When Leasing Still Makes Sense

If you're not confident about a specific EV's long-term resale value, want to upgrade to newer battery and charging tech every few years, or the automaker is currently running a strong lease-specific incentive independent of the old federal credit, leasing remains a reasonable choice — just don't assume it comes with an automatic tax-credit discount anymore. Compare the actual lease terms on offer against a real loan quote for the same vehicle before deciding.

Common Mistakes With This Decision in 2026

  • Assuming a lease still carries a hidden $7,500 discount. That specific mechanism (the 45W commercial credit pass-through) ended September 30, 2025. Any discount you see today is either a manufacturer incentive or ordinary lease math.
  • Comparing sticker lease payment to sticker loan payment without adjusting for equity. A lease payment that looks $150/month cheaper isn't necessarily the better deal if you'd otherwise keep the car 7+ years and build real equity in it.
  • Ignoring mileage caps until the lease-end bill arrives. If you drive more than a typical commute, run your actual annual mileage against the lease's included allowance before signing.
  • Not checking whether the vehicle qualifies for the new loan-interest deduction. It only applies to a purchase with a loan, on a new US-assembled vehicle — worth checking before you decide between buying and leasing the same car.
